Simple Measurament with 5 qubits

Experiments

We have to consider the following "experiments":
  • #0 : $\Psi_1,\Psi_1,\Psi_1,\Psi_1,\Psi_1$
  • #1 : $\Psi_1,\Psi_1,\Psi_1,\Psi_1,\Psi_2$
  • #2 : $\Psi_1,\Psi_1,\Psi_1,\Psi_2,\Psi_2$
  • #3 : $\Psi_1,\Psi_1,\Psi_2,\Psi_2,\Psi_2$
  • #4 : $\Psi_1,\Psi_2,\Psi_2,\Psi_2,\Psi_2$
  • #5 : $\Psi_2,\Psi_2,\Psi_2,\Psi_2,\Psi_2$
Remember that our strategy is:
  • $\Pi_1 \rightarrow \Psi_1$
  • $\Pi_2 \rightarrow \Psi_2$

Experiment #0

In that case when we measure we will get always the outcome $\Pi_1$ (because $P(\Pi_2|\Psi_1)=0$) and our "bet" is $\Psi_1$ son 100% success.

Experiment #1

The four first qubits are like the previous one but in the last ($\Psi_2$) we can get:
  • $\Pi_1$ $\rightarrow$ we will "bet" for $\Psi_1$ $\rightarrow$ Fail
  • $\Pi_2$ $\rightarrow$ we will "bet" for $\Psi_2$ $\rightarrow$ Ok
How often do we suceed? This is the same than asking the probability of measuring $\Pi_2 \rightarrow P(\Pi_2|\Psi_2) = 1 - c^2$
So the probability of success is $1-c^2$

Experiment #2

The stragtegy is the same, but in that case there are 2 states $\Psi_2$ so the probability of success is $(1-c^2)^2$.

Experiment #n

So in genertal if we have $n$ particles $\Psi_2$ the probability of success is $(1-c^2)^n$

Some examples

In the case the $c=0$ the probability of sucess is $(1-c^2)^n = 1$. This is because in this case both states are ortogonal and perfectly distinguible.
In the case the $c \approx 1$ the probability of sucess is $(1-c^2)^n \approx 0$. This is because in this case both states are almost equal and can not be distinguished.
